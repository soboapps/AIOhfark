*Farkle, or Farkel, is a dice game that has also been called or is similar to 1000/5000/10000, Cosmic Wimpout, Greed, Hot Dice, Squelch, Zilch, Zonk, or Darsh to name but a few. Its origins as a folk game are unknown, but it has been marketed commercially since 1982. While the basic rules are well-established, there is a wide range of variation in both scoring and play, as described below.
 

How to Play
OhFark is played by two players, with each player in succession having a turn at rolling the dice. Each player's turn results in a score, and the scores for each player accumulate to some winning total (usually 10,000).
 

Rules
  • At the beginning of each turn, the player rolls the dice.
  • After each roll, one or more scoring dice must be selected (see sections on scoring).
  • The player may then either end their turn and bank the score accumulated so far, or continue to roll the remaining dice.
  • If the player has scored all six dice, they have "hot dice" and may continue their turn with a new roll of all six dice, adding to the score they have already accumulated. There is no limit to the number of "hot dice" a player may roll in one turn.
  • If none of the dice score in any given roll, the player has "farkled" and all points for that turn are lost.
  • At the end of the player's turn, the dice are passed to the the next player.

Once a player has achieved a winning point total, the other player has one last turn to score enough points to surpass that high-score.
 

Standard scoring

For example, if a player rolls 1-2-3-3-3-5, they could do any of the following:

  • score three 3s as 300 and then roll the remaining three dice
  • score the single 1 as 100 and then roll the remaining five dice
  • score the single 5 as 50 and then roll the remaining five dice
  • score three 3s, the single 1, and the single 5 for a total of 450 and then roll the remaining die
  • score three 3s, the single 1, and the single 5 for a total of 450 and stop, banking 450 points in that turn

This is not an exhaustive list of plays based on that roll, but it covers the most likely ones. If the player continues rolling, as in any of the above cases except the last, they risk farkling and thus losing all accumulated points. On the other hand, if they score five dice and have only one die to roll, they have a 1 in 3 chance of scoring a single 1 or a single 5, and then having scored all six dice they will have "hot dice" and can roll all six dice again to further increase their score.

Each scoring combination must be achieved in a single roll. For example, if a player has already selected two individual 1s and then rolls a third with the four dice remaining, they do not have a triplet of 1s for a score of 1000 but merely three individual 1s for a score of 300.

Since farkle is a folk game, variant rules are used in different playing communities. While the standard rules described above are widely used, even they are not universal. For example, Three pair (e.g., 1-1-4-4-6-6) is scored as 500, 600, 750, 1000, or 1500. A straight (1-2-3-4-5-6) is scored as 1000, 1200, 1500, or 2500. Four, five and six of a kind are scored in one of 3 ways: adding, doubling or set value: Adding, for each additional matching die above 3 of a kind, the 3 of a kind score is added. e.g. 3-3-3 = 300, 3-3-3-3 = 300 + 300 (600), 3-3-3-3-3 = 300 + 300 + 300 (900) and 3-3-3-3-3-3 = 300 + 300 + 300 + 300 (1200).
Doubling, for each additional matching die above 3, 4 or 5 of a kind the score is doubled. e.g. 3-3-3 = 300, 3-3-3-3 = 300 × 2 (600), 3-3-3-3-3 = 300 × 2 × 2 (1200) and 3-3-3-3-3-3 = 300 × 2 × 2 × 2 (2400) Set value, 4 of a kind is scored as 1000 or 2000, 5 of a kind is scored as 2000 or 4000 and 6 of a kind is scored as 3000, 6000 or 10000.

Like the standard combinations, any of these variant combinations must be achieved in a single roll. These are the variations listed in the above-cited descriptions of farkle scoring, but further variations presumably exist.
